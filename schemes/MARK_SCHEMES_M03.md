### QUESTION
Discuss the prevention, antenatal diagnosis and management of red cell (rhesus D) alloimmunisation in pregnancy. (100 marks)

### SECTION 1 — Booking/28-week screening, informed consent & anti-D prophylaxis (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Prevention begins with universal, consented ABO/D grouping and red cell antibody screening at booking (around 10+0 weeks) and again at 28 weeks' gestation in all pregnant women, D-positive or D-negative, since D-positive women can also develop clinically significant non-D antibodies later in pregnancy. In RhD-negative women, the 28-week sample must be taken before RAADP is given, and routine antenatal anti-D prophylaxis is then offered to those not yet sensitised. A key modern challenge is distinguishing passive anti-D (from prophylaxis) from immune anti-D, since misclassifying either direction can leave a woman unprotected or a fetus inadequately monitored; the distinction rests on trend over time and quantification against the NIBSC anti-D standard rather than a single reading.
points:
- [2] All pregnant women, whether D-positive or D-negative, tested for ABO/D group and red cell antibody screen at booking (~10+0 weeks) and again at 28 weeks' gestation | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 5.1
- [2] D-positive women are just as likely as D-negative women to form clinically significant antibodies (other than anti-D) late in pregnancy | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 5.1
- [2] In D-negative women, the 28-week antibody screening sample must be taken before the first dose of RAADP is administered, to avoid confusing passive anti-D with immune anti-D | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 5.1
- [2] If no clinically significant antibodies are present at 28 weeks, no further routine antenatal blood grouping/antibody screening is necessary | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 5.1
- [2] Approximately 1% of pregnant women have clinically significant red cell antibodies, of which anti-D remains the most common specificity | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 4.2
- [3] Routine antenatal anti-D prophylaxis (RAADP) offered to all non-sensitised RhD-negative women, either as a single dose or as two doses across the third trimester | NICE TA156; BSH guideline for the use of anti-D Ig (Qureshi et al. 2014, cited as reference 2 within Regan et al. 2025)
- [2] Passive anti-D Ig and immune anti-D cannot be qualitatively distinguished by serology alone; they are distinguished by trend over time — passive concentration falls, immune concentration remains stable or rises | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.1
- [2] Half-life of passive anti-D Ig is approximately 3 weeks; immune anti-D becomes detectable around 4 weeks after primary exposure and reaches peak concentration at 6–8 weeks | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.1
- [2] Concentration of passive anti-D Ig rarely exceeds 0.4 IU/mL unless more than 1500 IU has been administered | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.1
- [3] Anti-D detected at or before the 28-week RAADP sample, or whose passive/immune nature cannot be confirmed, must be monitored as though immune until proven otherwise | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.2, Figure 3
- [3] The 2011 Serious Hazards of Transfusion (SHOT) report identified cases where immune anti-D was wrongly assumed to be passive, leading to inadequate follow-up and babies born with HDFN — underlining the clinical importance of correct interpretation | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1
calibration: Must-have — universal booking + 28-week screening in ALL women (not just RhD-negative), the requirement to sample before RAADP, and explicit recognition that passive and immune anti-D cannot be distinguished on a single result. Figures that must be exact: passive anti-D half-life ~3 weeks; immune anti-D detectable ~4 weeks, peak 6–8 weeks; passive concentration rarely >0.4 IU/mL (unless >1500 IU given). Common omissions: candidates screen only D-negative women rather than all women, and fail to address the passive-vs-immune anti-D distinction at all.

### SECTION 2 — Sensitising events, fetomaternal haemorrhage testing & postnatal management (20 raw → 4.0 scaled)
model: Anti-D Ig is given for potentially sensitising events in non-sensitised RhD-negative women, and a fetomaternal haemorrhage (FMH) test performed at delivery (and repeated if needed) to determine whether additional dosing is required. At birth, cord blood is used to establish the neonate's D group and, where an immune maternal antibody is present, to perform a direct antiglobulin test (DAT) and check haemoglobin/bilirubin with clinical observation for jaundice; a routine DAT is not indicated in D-negative women with no alloantibodies, since passive anti-D crossing the placenta causes a positive DAT in 3–6% of cases without clinical significance. Babies transfused in utero may type as D-negative for months, so ongoing haemoglobin/bilirubin surveillance for late red cell destruction (up to 6–8 weeks) is still required.
points:
- [3] D-negative women with no immune anti-D should have an initial FMH test at delivery, with follow-up testing if required, to determine any need for additional anti-D dosing | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.3
- [2] Anti-D Ig given following any potentially sensitising event in a non-sensitised RhD-negative woman (e.g. invasive prenatal testing), in accordance with the BSH guideline for anti-D Ig use | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 10.3, referencing Qureshi et al. 2014
- [2] Cord blood sample obtained at delivery to determine the baby's D group where not already known, identifying women who require postnatal anti-D Ig | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.1
- [2] Routine DAT on cord samples of D-positive babies born to D-negative women with no red cell alloantibodies is not recommended, as passive anti-D Ig crossing the placenta produces a positive DAT in 3–6% of cases without causing clinically significant haemolysis | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.2.1
- [3] Where maternal serum contains an immune, IAT-reactive red cell antibody, cord red cells are tested for the corresponding antigen, a DAT is performed, and haemoglobin/bilirubin are checked, with the baby observed clinically for jaundice | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.2.2
- [2] A positive DAT is not, in itself, diagnostic of HDFN; where the DAT is positive and the baby shows signs of HDFN, a red cell eluate may help confirm the antibody specificity responsible | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.2.2
- [2] Babies transfused in utero with D-negative units may type as D-negative for several months after birth; neonatal haemoglobin/bilirubin should still be checked to exclude significant late red cell destruction, which can occur up to 6–8 weeks after birth | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.2.2
- [2] For women with no red cell antibodies at 28 weeks, routine pre-/post-delivery blood grouping and antibody screening is unnecessary unless pre-transfusion compatibility testing is required | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.3
- [2] In alloimmunised pregnancies, a pre- or post-delivery maternal sample is required to detect any newly formed antibodies and guide crossmatching in case neonatal transfusion is needed | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 9.3
calibration: Must-have safety points — FMH/Kleihauer testing at delivery to guide additional anti-D dosing, and the correct indications for cord DAT (only when an immune maternal antibody is present, not routinely). Common omissions: candidates recommend routine DAT on all D-negative deliveries (incorrect per current guidance), and forget that late anaemia surveillance is still needed after in-utero transfusion even when the baby types as D-negative.

### SECTION 3 — Antibody-specific risk stratification and antenatal surveillance (30 raw → 6.0 scaled)
model: Anti-D, anti-c and anti-K are the antibodies most often implicated in HDFN severe enough to warrant antenatal intervention, and any woman with a previous baby affected by significant HDFN is referred to a fetal medicine specialist before 20 weeks regardless of current antibody level. Anti-D and anti-c are risk-stratified using validated quantification thresholds and monitored at least every 4 weeks to 28 weeks and fortnightly thereafter, while anti-K is referred to fetal medicine on first identification irrespective of titre because its severity correlates poorly with maternal antibody strength. Fetal genotyping by cffDNA and serial MCA-PSV Doppler (threshold >1.5 MoM) together determine whether the fetus is at risk and how closely it needs to be watched.
points:
- [2] Anti-D, anti-c and anti-K are the antibodies most often implicated in causing HDFN severe enough to warrant antenatal intervention | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6
- [3] Women with a previous history of confirmed significant HDFN are referred to a fetal medicine specialist before 20 weeks' gestation, irrespective of current antibody concentration or specificity | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6, Recommendation
- [3] Immune anti-D is quantified against the NIBSC standard: <4 IU/mL — HDFN unlikely, continue to monitor; 4–15 IU/mL — moderate risk, refer to a fetal medicine specialist; >15 IU/mL — high risk, urgent referral | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3, Table 1
- [2] Immune anti-c is similarly quantified: <7.5 IU/mL unlikely to cause HDFN; 7.5–20 IU/mL moderate risk; >20 IU/mL high risk requiring urgent referral | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.2, Table 2
- [2] Immune anti-D and anti-c are retested at least every 4 weeks to 28 weeks' gestation and every 2 weeks thereafter until delivery | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Sections 6.1.3, 6.2
- [2] Anti-K (and other Kell system antibodies) are referred to a fetal medicine specialist on first identification regardless of titre, owing to a less predictable, titre-independent relationship between antibody level and severity of fetal anaemia | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.3
- [2] HDFN due to anti-K arises predominantly from suppression of fetal erythroid progenitor cells rather than haemolysis, producing fetal anaemia without the elevated bilirubin typically seen with anti-D/anti-c | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.3
- [2] Anti-K is titrated monthly to 28 weeks and fortnightly thereafter; a titre of 1:4 or greater is a recommended cut-off for commencing serial MCA Doppler surveillance | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.3
- [2] Non-invasive fetal genotyping (cffDNA) for RHD, RHCE (c/E) or KEL*01 (K) is performed from maternal plasma to establish fetal antigen status, with a false-negative rate of less than 1% | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 4.6.1
- [2] Fetal K genotyping samples should not be taken before 20 weeks' gestation, whereas RHD/RHCE genotyping is reliable in alloimmunised pregnancies from 16 weeks | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 4.6.1
- [2] Paternal phenotype/genotype testing (heterozygous vs homozygous) is considered to predict fetal risk, particularly where non-invasive fetal genotyping for the relevant antigen is unavailable | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 4.5
- [4] Middle cerebral artery peak systolic velocity (MCA-PSV) Doppler is used to monitor for and diagnose fetal anaemia; MCA-PSV greater than 1.5 multiples of the median (MoM) strongly correlates with moderate-to-severe fetal anaemia | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3
- [2] MCA-PSV surveillance is performed weekly when the indication is a critical/rising antibody concentration, and at least fortnightly when the indication is a previous history of HDFN; MCA Doppler becomes less accurate for assessing fetal anaemia after 35 weeks' gestation | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3
- [2] Clinically significant antibodies other than anti-D, -c or -K are titrated at booking and at 28 weeks; a titre of 32 or greater and/or a past history of HDFN prompts referral to a fetal medicine specialist | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.4
calibration: Must-have safety points — the exact anti-D/anti-c quantification thresholds (Tables 1 and 2), explicit recognition that anti-K is managed by titre-independent referral rather than a concentration threshold, and the MCA-PSV >1.5 MoM criterion with its reduced reliability after 35 weeks. Common omissions: candidates apply the anti-D concentration thresholds to anti-K (incorrect), and fail to mention that referral for a previous HDFN history is independent of the current pregnancy's antibody level.

### SECTION 4 — Management of the affected fetus/neonate: intrauterine transfusion, blood requirements, delivery & outcomes (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Intrauterine transfusion (IUT) is the definitive treatment for significant fetal anaemia (MCA-PSV consistently >1.5 MoM), performed in a tertiary centre with fetal blood sampling immediately beforehand to confirm haematocrit, using irradiated, plasma-reduced blood with only a 24-hour shelf life; most units do not transfuse beyond 34 weeks. Delivery timing balances antibody-exposure duration against prematurity, with delivery no later than 37–38 weeks unless rising antibody levels or IUT requirements dictate earlier birth, and mode of delivery follows standard obstetric indications rather than being mandated by IUT itself. Neonatal transfusion requirements (exchange vs top-up) carry specific antigen-negative and irradiation requirements, and late neonatal anaemia — particularly with anti-c or after multiple IUTs — needs ongoing surveillance, though long-term neurodevelopmental outcome after IUT is reassuringly comparable to the general population.
points:
- [4] Intrauterine transfusion is indicated where MCA-PSV is consistently greater than 1.5 MoM, and is performed in a tertiary centre with expertise in invasive fetal therapy | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3
- [2] Fetal blood sampling is performed immediately before IUT to assess fetal haematocrit, with preparation in place to proceed seamlessly to transfusion | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3
- [3] Serial IUTs may be required depending on the gestational age at diagnosis of fetal anaemia; most clinicians do not perform IUT beyond 34 weeks' gestation | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3
- [3] Blood for IUT is irradiated (to prevent transfusion-associated graft-versus-host disease), plasma-reduced to increase the haematocrit to 0.70–0.85, and has only a 24-hour shelf life after processing | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 8.2
- [4] Delivery of a fetus at risk of HDFN is timed no later than 37–38 weeks' gestation to minimise duration of exposure to maternal antibody, unless earlier delivery is indicated by rising antibody levels/titres or ongoing IUT requirements | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 6.1.3
- [2] Mode of delivery is determined by standard obstetric indications; IUT does not, of itself, mandate delivery by caesarean section | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 10.4
- [2] Continuous electronic fetal monitoring is advised in labour, given these are high-risk pregnancies | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 10.4
- [2] Delayed/late neonatal anaemia can occur postnatally; recognised risk factors include anti-c antibodies and multiple intrauterine transfusions | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 10.5
- [2] Long-term neurodevelopmental outcome after IUT for HDFN is comparable to the general population, whereas severe untreated hydrops is independently associated with neurodevelopmental impairment — reinforcing the value of timely IUT | BSH Guideline (Regan et al. 2025), Section 10.5, citing the LOTUS study
calibration: Must-have safety points — the MCA-PSV >1.5 MoM trigger for IUT, irradiation requirement for IUT and any post-IUT neonatal transfusion, and the 37–38 week delivery ceiling. Figures that must be exact: IUT blood haematocrit 0.70–0.85 with 24-hour shelf life; exchange transfusion blood <5 days old; top-up transfusion blood up to 35 days old. Common omissions: candidates forget that top-up transfusion blood does not need irradiation unless there was a prior IUT, and omit the reassuring long-term neurodevelopmental data used to counsel women about IUT.
