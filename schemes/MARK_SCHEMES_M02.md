### QUESTION
Venous thromboembolism is a leading cause of direct maternal death. Discuss the assessment of risk and the strategies you would use to prevent venous thromboembolism during pregnancy and the puerperium. (100 marks)

### SECTION 1 — Epidemiology, pathophysiology & classification of risk factors (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Venous thromboembolism (VTE) remains a leading direct cause of maternal death, reflecting the hypercoagulable state of pregnancy (increased clotting factors, reduced protein S, venous stasis from progesterone-mediated venodilation and mechanical compression by the gravid uterus). Risk assessment is structured around pre-existing factors, obstetric factors, and new-onset/transient factors, since VTE risk changes dynamically across pregnancy, delivery and the puerperium and must be reassessed at each of these points.
points:
- [2] VTE remains one of the leading direct causes of maternal death in the UK and globally | RCOG Green-top Guideline No. 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state — increased levels of factors VII, VIII, X and fibrinogen, and reduced protein S activity | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Venous stasis increases due to progesterone-mediated venodilation and mechanical compression of pelvic veins by the gravid uterus | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Risk of VTE is greatest in the immediate postpartum period, particularly the first 3 weeks | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Risk assessment should be performed at first antenatal booking | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Risk reassessed if admitted to hospital for any reason, and again intrapartum/immediately postpartum | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Pre-existing risk factors: previous VTE, known thrombophilia (inherited or acquired) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Pre-existing risk factors: medical comorbidity — active cancer, heart failure, SLE, inflammatory bowel disease, nephrotic syndrome, sickle cell disease, IV drug use | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Pre-existing risk factors: age >35 years, BMI ≥30 kg/m², parity ≥3, smoker, gross varicose veins | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Obstetric risk factors: multiple pregnancy, current pre-eclampsia, assisted reproduction/IVF pregnancy | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Obstetric risk factors: caesarean section (especially emergency in labour), prolonged labour, mid-cavity/rotational operative delivery, stillbirth, preterm birth, PPH >1 litre or transfusion | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Transient/new-onset risk factors: any surgical procedure in pregnancy or puerperium, hyperemesis/dehydration, ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Transient/new-onset risk factors: current systemic infection, immobility (≥3 days bed rest), long-haul travel (>4 hours) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Thrombophilias are classified into high-risk (e.g. antithrombin deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome, combined/homozygous defects) and lower-risk (e.g. heterozygous factor V Leiden or prothrombin gene mutation) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Individual risk factors are cumulative — accumulation of multiple lower-risk factors raises overall risk substantially, which is why formal points-based scoring is used rather than gestalt assessment | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
calibration: Must-have points: explicit statement that risk assessment must be dynamic (booking, admission, delivery, postpartum) and the three-tier classification (pre-existing / obstetric / transient). Common omission: candidates describe risk factors as an unstructured list without naming the three categories, and forget that risk must be reassessed at every hospital admission, not just at booking.

### SECTION 2 — Antenatal risk assessment & thromboprophylaxis strategy (40 raw → 8.0 scaled)
model: A structured, points-based risk assessment at booking determines who requires antenatal thromboprophylaxis and from what gestation. Women with previous VTE or high-risk thrombophilia require LMWH throughout pregnancy regardless of other factors. Those accumulating ≥4 risk-score points require LMWH from the first trimester, and those with 3 points from 28 weeks. LMWH is the agent of choice, dosed by booking weight, with mechanical methods reserved for those in whom anticoagulants are contraindicated.
points:
- [3] Women with a previous single VTE (excluding an isolated event related to major surgery) require LMWH thromboprophylaxis throughout the antenatal period | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] Women with previous VTE and a high-risk thrombophilia, or on long-term anticoagulation, require higher-intensity/therapeutic-dose LMWH with specialist input | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Women with asymptomatic high-risk thrombophilia (e.g. antithrombin deficiency) should be considered for antenatal LMWH even without a personal history of VTE | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] A formal risk-scoring system is applied at booking: risk factors are assigned points (e.g. 1–2 points each) and summed | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] Four or more risk-score points antenatally → LMWH thromboprophylaxis from the first trimester | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] Three risk-score points antenatally → LMWH thromboprophylaxis from 28 weeks of gestation | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Women admitted antenatally for any reason, or developing a new transient risk factor, should have their score reassessed and LMWH considered even if not previously indicated | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] LMWH (e.g. enoxaparin, dalteparin, tinzaparin) is the agent of choice for antenatal thromboprophylaxis — safe in pregnancy and breastfeeding, does not cross the placenta | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] Dosing is weight-based, using early-pregnancy or booking weight, with defined dose bands and increased doses at extremes of body weight | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Avoid warfarin in pregnancy other than in specific situations (e.g. some women with mechanical heart valves) because of teratogenicity and fetal/maternal haemorrhagic risk | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) are not recommended in pregnancy or breastfeeding | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Mechanical thromboprophylaxis (graduated compression stockings, intermittent pneumatic compression) considered antenatally where LMWH is contraindicated (e.g. active bleeding, high bleeding risk) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Women should be taught self-injection technique and counselled on correct administration and adherence | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Advise on general risk-reduction measures — adequate hydration, avoiding prolonged immobility, mobilisation | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Plan for peripartum management of LMWH in advance — timing of last dose relative to induction/caesarean section and neuraxial anaesthesia | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] LMWH should be omitted on the day of planned induction/delivery, with clear communication to the woman and the anaesthetic team | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Regional anaesthesia/analgesia should not be sited within 12 hours of a prophylactic dose of LMWH (24 hours for a treatment dose) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Testing for thrombophilia is not routinely recommended in an unselected population but may be considered where the result would change management | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
calibration: Must-have safety points: correct identification of previous-VTE/high-risk thrombophilia as an automatic indication for antenatal LMWH, the score thresholds (4 points → first trimester, 3 points → 28 weeks), and the neuraxial-anaesthesia timing rule relative to LMWH dosing. Figures that must be exact: 4 vs 3 point thresholds and corresponding gestations; 12-hour (prophylactic) / 24-hour (treatment) neuraxial interval. Common omissions: candidates omit weight-based dosing, forget to mention DOACs/warfarin are contraindicated, and rarely mention pre-planning the peripartum LMWH-anaesthesia interval.

### SECTION 3 — Postnatal risk assessment & additional preventive strategies (35 raw → 7.0 scaled)
model: All women require a fresh VTE risk assessment following birth, since delivery itself is a major risk factor and additional postnatal-specific factors (caesarean section, prolonged labour, PPH) apply. Any woman with ≥2 persisting risk factors, or a single higher-weighted factor, should receive postnatal LMWH for a minimum of 10 days, extended to 6 weeks in higher-risk women, alongside mechanical measures, mobilisation, and institutional-level strategies such as universal risk assessment and staff education.
points:
- [3] Postnatal risk assessment repeated after every birth, since delivery itself confers additional risk regardless of antenatal score | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] All women having a caesarean section should be considered for postnatal LMWH thromboprophylaxis | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] Women with any one higher-weighted risk factor (e.g. BMI ≥40, prolonged admission, postpartum haemorrhage requiring transfusion) or ≥2 lower-weighted risk factors should receive postnatal LMWH for a minimum of 10 days | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [3] Women with previous VTE, high-risk thrombophilia, or multiple persisting risk factors should continue LMWH for 6 weeks postnatally | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Postnatal LMWH should be commenced as soon as feasible after delivery, balancing bleeding risk (e.g. after PPH or neuraxial anaesthesia removal) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] First postnatal dose timed with reference to removal of epidural/spinal catheter (e.g. at least 4 hours after removal) | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] LMWH is safe in breastfeeding and does not require discontinuation | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Warfarin may be used postnatally as an alternative in selected women, and is compatible with breastfeeding | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Encourage early mobilisation after vaginal or caesarean birth as a simple, universally applicable preventive measure | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Graduated compression stockings/intermittent pneumatic compression as an adjunct or alternative where LMWH is contraindicated | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [1] Adequate hydration and avoidance of prolonged bed rest postnatally | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Clear written information given to women on signs and symptoms of VTE and when to seek urgent review | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Institutional strategy: universal, structured risk-assessment proforma completed and documented at booking, admission, and postnatally for every woman | RCOG Green-top 37a (latest edition) [VERIFY]
- [2] Institutional strategy: regular staff training/audit on VTE risk assessment and thromboprophylaxis prescribing | RCOG/local governance guidance (latest edition) [SLCOG-CHECK]
- [2] Suspected VTE should prompt objective testing and treatment-dose anticoagulation without waiting for confirmation, given the consequences of a missed diagnosis | RCOG Green-top 37b — acute management of VTE in pregnancy (latest edition) [VERIFY]

### QUESTION
Critically evaluate the diagnosis and management of endometrial hyperplasia and early endometrial carcinoma. (100 marks)

---

### SECTION 1 — Classification, epidemiology & risk stratification (15 raw → 3.0 scaled)
model: Endometrial hyperplasia is classified by the WHO 2014 system into hyperplasia without atypia and atypical hyperplasia (interchangeable with EIN), arising from unopposed oestrogen action. Endometrial carcinoma is classified histologically (endometrioid vs non-endometrioid) and, increasingly, by TCGA molecular subgroup (POLE, MMRd, p53abn, NSMP), which refines prognosis beyond FIGO stage. Histological confirmation is mandatory before any definitive treatment decision.
points:
- [3] WHO 2014 classification: hyperplasia without atypia vs atypical hyperplasia (atypia = EIN) | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Hyperplasia arises from oestrogen unopposed by progesterone stimulating endometrial proliferation | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [3] Risk factors: obesity, PCOS/chronic anovulation, tamoxifen, unopposed oestrogen/HRT, granulosa cell tumour | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Endometrial carcinoma classified as endometrioid (Type 1) vs non-endometrioid — serous, clear cell, carcinosarcoma (Type 2) | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
- [3] TCGA molecular subgroups — POLEmut (favourable), MMRd, p53abn (unfavourable), NSMP — increasingly used for prognosis/treatment [VERIFY — confirm if SLCOG mandates molecular testing locally] | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
- [2] Histological confirmation is required before definitive treatment planning in all suspected cases | SLCOG Guideline 2021
calibration: Candidates must distinguish hyperplasia (premalignant) from carcinoma (malignant) clearly and use correct WHO terminology. Common omission: failing to mention molecular classification as a modern prognostic addition, and confusing simple/complex (1994 WHO, obsolete) with the current atypia-based system.

### SECTION 2 — Diagnostic assessment (20 raw → 4.0 scaled)
model: Diagnosis of both hyperplasia and carcinoma requires histological sampling, usually by outpatient endometrial biopsy, with hysteroscopy reserved for non-diagnostic or failed sampling. Once carcinoma is confirmed, staging investigations (clinical pelvic exam, CXR, ultrasound/CT/MRI as available) assess local and distant spread and fitness for surgery, ideally discussed in a multidisciplinary setting.
points:
- [3] Endometrial biopsy (outpatient sampling) is the primary diagnostic test for both hyperplasia and carcinoma | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Hysteroscopy ± directed biopsy indicated where outpatient sampling fails, is non-diagnostic, or a focal lesion (polyp) is suspected | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [3] Transvaginal ultrasound has a role in triaging postmenopausal bleeding; endometrial thickness cut-offs reduce probability of cancer [VERIFY exact cut-off currently used locally] | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Speculum and per-vaginal examination to assess cervical/vaginal/parametrial involvement in all confirmed cancer cases | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Chest X-ray (minimum) and abdomino-pelvic ultrasound (if cross-sectional imaging unavailable) in all patients | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [3] MRI pelvis (where available) to assess myometrial invasion, cervical stromal/parametrial involvement and pelvic nodal enlargement | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [3] CECT chest/abdomen/pelvis for suspected Stage III/IV disease or poorly-differentiated histology (clear cell, serous, MMMT, Grade 3 endometrioid) | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Full blood count, renal and liver function to assess fitness for surgery; MDT discussion before intervention where facilities allow | SLCOG Guideline 2021
calibration: Must-have safety point: histological diagnosis before treatment. Common omission: candidates jump straight to surgery/treatment without describing the staged diagnostic work-up (imaging tailored to risk, not universal CT for all).

### SECTION 3 — Management of endometrial hyperplasia without atypia (15 raw → 3.0 scaled)
model: Hyperplasia without atypia has a low (<5% over 20 years) risk of progression to cancer, and most cases regress spontaneously. Reversible risk factors should be addressed, with the LNG-IUS as first-line medical treatment given superior regression rates over oral progestogens. Surgery is reserved for treatment failure, relapse, or patient preference against surveillance.
points:
- [3] Address reversible risk factors — weight loss advice, review of HRT regimen | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] LNG-IUS is first-line medical treatment (higher regression rate, better bleeding profile than oral progestogens) | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [3] Continuous oral progestogens (e.g. medroxyprogesterone 10–20 mg/day) if LNG-IUS declined; cyclical progestogens should not be used | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Minimum 6 months' treatment; endometrial surveillance biopsies at least 6-monthly, ≥2 consecutive negative biopsies before discharge | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [3] Hysterectomy indicated if no regression after 12 months, relapse after treatment, persistent bleeding, or non-compliance with surveillance | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Endometrial ablation is not recommended (destruction cannot be ensured; precludes future histological surveillance) | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
calibration: Safety-critical point: LNG-IUS as first-line, not observation alone, in symptomatic women. Common omission: forgetting the 12-month rule for escalation to hysterectomy, and forgetting ablation is contraindicated.

### SECTION 4 — Management of atypical hyperplasia (15 raw → 3.0 scaled)
model: Atypical hyperplasia carries a high risk of concurrent or progressive malignancy (up to ~43% concurrent carcinoma at hysterectomy), so total hysterectomy is the standard of care, preferably laparoscopic. Fertility-sparing management with progestogens is reserved for carefully selected, counselled women under MDT supervision, with close surveillance.
points:
- [3] Total hysterectomy is recommended given risk of underlying/progressive malignancy (concurrent carcinoma reported in up to 43% of hysterectomy specimens) | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Laparoscopic approach preferred over abdominal (shorter stay, less pain, quicker recovery) | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [3] No benefit from intraoperative frozen section or routine lymphadenectomy in atypical hyperplasia | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [2] Bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy in peri-/postmenopausal women; individualise ovarian conservation in premenopausal women (consider salpingectomy) | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
- [3] Fertility-sparing treatment (LNG-IUS first-line, oral progestogens second-line) only after MDT review, imaging and tumour markers to exclude invasive/ovarian cancer, in carefully counselled women wishing to preserve fertility | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016); SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Endometrial surveillance every 3 months until ≥2 consecutive negative biopsies if managed conservatively | RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016)
calibration: Must-have safety point: hysterectomy is the standard even though regression rates with progestogens can be high — fertility preservation is non-standard and must be explicitly flagged as inferior. Common omission: not mentioning the high concurrent-cancer risk as the rationale for surgery.

### SECTION 5 — Management of early (Stage I–II) endometrial carcinoma (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Standard surgical treatment for Stage I disease is total hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy, tailored by grade and myometrial invasion, with pelvic ± para-aortic lymphadenectomy reserved for high-risk features. Stage II disease may be managed by simple hysterectomy with full lymph node staging and adjuvant therapy, avoiding routine radical hysterectomy. Adjuvant radiotherapy and/or chemotherapy is stratified by post-operative risk group, and molecular classification is increasingly used to individualise this decision.
points:
- [3] Total hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy is the standard surgical treatment for Stage I disease | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Both open and laparoscopic routes acceptable; laparoscopic surgery requires preoperative MRI and careful patient selection to avoid morcellation/spillage | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [3] Pelvic ± para-aortic lymphadenectomy recommended for high-risk features (Grade 3, >50% myometrial invasion, Type 2 histology); total pelvic nodal count >10 recommended | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Sentinel lymph node mapping (e.g. with indocyanine green) is increasingly used as an alternative to systematic lymphadenectomy | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
- [3] Stage II: simple hysterectomy with bilateral pelvic ± para-aortic lymphadenectomy plus adjuvant therapy is now favoured over radical hysterectomy without adjuvant therapy | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Post-operative risk stratification (low/intermediate/high) determines referral for adjuvant treatment; all Stage II–IV disease referred to clinical oncology | SLCOG Guideline 2021; RCOG/BSGE Green-top 67 (2016), Table 3
- [3] Vaginal brachytherapy is standard adjuvant treatment for high-intermediate risk Stage I–II disease (excellent vaginal control, minimal toxicity) | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
- [2] Pelvic external beam radiotherapy reserved for higher-risk Stage I–II disease (grade 3 with deep invasion and/or LVSI, unfavourable histology/molecular factors) | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
- [3] Adjuvant chemotherapy (± radiotherapy) considered for serous/p53-abnormal cancers and Stage III disease | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
- [2] Molecular classification (POLEmut/MMRd/p53abn/NSMP) is increasingly incorporated to individualise adjuvant treatment and avoid over/undertreatment [VERIFY — local SLCOG adoption of molecular-guided adjuvant therapy] | FIGO Cancer Report 2021
calibration: Must-have safety points: correct standard surgery (TH+BSO), and the principle that adjuvant therapy is risk-stratified, not universal. Figures that must be exact: nodal count >10, brachytherapy as standard for high-intermediate risk. Common omission: candidates over-apply radical hysterectomy to Stage II disease, or omit sentinel node mapping/molecular classification as evolving practice.

### SECTION 6 — Follow-up, fertility preservation & governance (10 raw → 2.0 scaled)
model: Follow-up frequency is risk-based, more intensive in the first two years for intermediate/high-risk disease. Fertility-sparing treatment (progestogens) may be offered in carefully selected young women with Grade 1 endometrioid cancer without myometrial invasion, always via MDT. Governance targets ensure timely diagnosis and treatment to optimise the generally favourable prognosis of early disease.
points:
- [3] Risk-based follow-up: low-risk 6-monthly for ≥2 years; intermediate/high-risk 3–4-monthly for first 2 years then 6-monthly (total 5 years) | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [3] Fertility-sparing treatment for Grade 1 endometrioid carcinoma without myometrial invasion: MDT selection, MRI to exclude invasion/nodal disease, MPA 400–600 mg/day, 3-monthly biopsy and 6-monthly MRI | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] Governance targets: histology report within 14 days of presentation with postmenopausal bleeding; definitive treatment within 6 weeks of biopsy | SLCOG Guideline 2021
- [2] HRT may be considered post-hysterectomy in low-risk disease (Grade 1/2 endometrioid, Stage IA, no LVSI) | SLCOG Guideline 2021
calibration: Must-have point: fertility preservation is a non-standard, time-limited option requiring MDT oversight, not a default. Common omission: omitting the 6-week treatment governance target, and omitting HRT eligibility criteria (grade/stage/LVSI-dependent).

### QUESTION
A 32-year-old woman, para 1, with a booking body mass index of 33 kg/m² is found to have a fasting plasma glucose of 6.0 mmol/L and a 2-hour value of 9.2 mmol/L on a 75 g oral glucose tolerance test performed at 26 weeks of gestation.

3.1. Justify the diagnosis and outline the initial management of gestational diabetes. **(25 marks)**
3.2. Describe the glycaemic targets and the antenatal fetal surveillance you would institute. **(25 marks)**
3.3. Discuss the considerations regarding the timing and mode of delivery. **(25 marks)**
3.4. Outline the intrapartum and immediate postnatal care, including neonatal considerations. **(25 marks)**

---

### SECTION 3.1 — Justify diagnosis and outline initial management (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: The diagnosis of GDM is confirmed by IADPSG/WHO criteria applied to the 24–28 week OGTT: her fasting value (108 mg/dL) and 2-hour value (166 mg/dL) both exceed threshold, and a single abnormal value is diagnostic. Her FPG remains below the diabetes-in-pregnancy cut-off, confirming standard (not overt) GDM, in a woman whose BMI of 33 kg/m² is itself a recognised risk factor. Initial management is multidisciplinary, beginning with individualised medical nutrition therapy and structured activity, with SMBG to guide the need for escalation to metformin or insulin within 1–2 weeks if targets are not met.

points:
- [2] IADPSG/WHO (2013) criteria, as adopted in the SLCOG/SLCE Hyperglycaemia in Pregnancy Guideline (2026), are used to diagnose GDM on the 24–28 week OGTT | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Diagnosis of GDM requires only ONE abnormal value on the 75 g OGTT | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Her fasting glucose of 6.0 mmol/L (108 mg/dL) exceeds the diagnostic threshold of ≥92 mg/dL (5.1 mmol/L) | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Her 2-hour glucose of 9.2 mmol/L (166 mg/dL) exceeds the diagnostic threshold of ≥153 mg/dL (8.5 mmol/L) | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] FPG remaining <126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L) excludes diabetes-in-pregnancy/overt diabetes, confirming standard GDM | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [1] Test was appropriately timed at 24–28 weeks as the routine rescreen after a presumed normal booking screen | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] BMI 33 kg/m² is a recognised risk factor supporting susceptibility to GDM | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Care should be delivered in a multidisciplinary set-up (obstetrician, endocrinologist/physician, dietitian, midwife) | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT) should be initiated immediately as first-line treatment | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] MNT alone achieves control in 80–90% of GDM cases | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Energy prescription individualised according to pre-pregnancy BMI category (SAFES-based targets) | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [1] Structured moderate-intensity physical activity advised (~30 min most days) | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [2] Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) initiated — fasting and postprandial checks | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)
- [1] Reassess glycaemic control within 1–2 weeks; escalate to metformin/insulin if targets unmet | SLCOG/SLCE HIP Guideline (2026)

### SECTION 3.2 — Glycaemic targets and antenatal fetal surveillance (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Targets for GDM not on insulin are fasting <95 mg/dL, 1-hour <140 mg/dL and 2-hour <120 mg/dL. ### SECTION 3.3 — Timing and mode of delivery (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Timing is individualised and finalised by 36 weeks according to treatment modality and complications: 40–40+6 weeks if diet-controlled and uncomplicated, 38–39 weeks on metformin, 37–38 weeks on insulin, and 36–37 weeks if complicated. Vaginal delivery by timed induction is the aim, as GDM alone is not an indication for caesarean section; estimated fetal weight >4.0 kg warrants discussion of risks and possible elective caesarean, while diabetes itself does not preclude VBAC/TOLAC.

### SECTION 3.4 — Intrapartum and immediate postnatal/neonatal care (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Intrapartum glucose should be kept at 72–126 mg/dL with hourly monitoring in active labour, using correctional subcutaneous insulin or VRIII according to control. All glucose-lowering treatment is stopped once the placenta is delivered, with 24-hour glucose monitoring to exclude persistent diabetes. The neonate requires glucose testing at 2–4 hours of age, early and frequent breastfeeding to prevent hypoglycaemia, and should remain in hospital until feeding and normoglycaemia are established, with the mother counselled about a 6–8 week postpartum OGTT and future risk.
