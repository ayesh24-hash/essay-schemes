## SCHEME_ID: M01-Q1
- topic: Maternal sepsis
- subject: OBS
- questionType: SAQ
- totalMarks: 20
- rawMarks: 100
- schemeVersion: 1.1

### QUESTION
Maternal sepsis remains an important cause of direct maternal death worldwide and in Sri Lanka. Discuss the measures that you would employ to reduce the maternal morbidity and mortality associated with maternal sepsis. (100 marks)

### SECTION 1 — Definition, burden, risk factors & common sources (25 raw → 5.0 scaled)
model: Maternal sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction (a SOFA-score rise of ≥2 points) resulting from infection during pregnancy, childbirth, abortion or the puerperium (up to 42 days), and remains a leading, largely preventable direct cause of maternal death, accounting for roughly 11% of maternal deaths globally. Deaths are driven by delayed recognition of a few common sources — genital tract, urinary tract, wound, breast and chest — with E. coli and Group A streptococcus the dominant organisms; pregnant women carry a markedly elevated risk of invasive GAS disease. Recognised risk factors include obesity, diabetes, anaemia, ethnic minority status, prolonged rupture of membranes and invasive procedures.

points:
- [2] Maternal sepsis is a leading direct cause of maternal death worldwide, accounting for ~11% of all maternal deaths | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), citing Say et al. Lancet Global Health (2014)
- [2] Most sepsis-related maternal deaths are preventable, with suboptimal care repeatedly identified in confidential enquiries | MBRRACE-UK 2019–21 report / RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [3] WHO definition: life-threatening condition defined as organ dysfunction resulting from infection during pregnancy, childbirth, post-abortion or postpartum period | WHO Statement on Maternal Sepsis (2017)
- [2] Postpartum period includes up to 42 days after termination of pregnancy, irrespective of cause | WHO (2017)
- [2] Sepsis-3 consensus: organ dysfunction defined as a SOFA score increase of ≥2 points; this makes formal diagnosis retrospective | Sepsis-3, Singer et al. JAMA (2016); RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Septic shock: persisting hypotension needing vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg plus lactate >2 mmol/l despite adequate fluids; mortality >40% | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] All women are at risk of sepsis in pregnancy and the postpartum period — a GPP recommendation, not confined to "high-risk" women | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Risk factor: obesity, diabetes in pregnancy, iron-deficiency anaemia, maternal age >35 | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 1
- [1] Risk factor: impaired immunity/immunosuppressant medication, renal/cardiac/liver disease, ethnic minority background | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 1
- [1] Risk factor: history of pelvic infection, close contact with iGAS, intravenous drug use | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 1
- [1] Risk factor: prolonged rupture of membranes, caesarean birth, vaginal trauma, retained products of conception | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 1
- [1] Risk factor: invasive procedures — amniocentesis, cervical cerclage, CVS; multiple gestation | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 1
- [1] Common source: genital tract — endometritis/chorioamnionitis/septic abortion | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Common source: wound infection — perineal/episiotomy/caesarean | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Common source: urinary tract infection/pyelonephritis | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Common source: mastitis/breast abscess — can occur antenatally as well as postnatally | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Common source: respiratory tract infection/pneumonia, including influenza and SARS-CoV-2 | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Pregnant women are at ~20-fold increased risk of invasive GAS compared with non-pregnant women, and ~80-fold up to 28 days postpartum; E. coli and GAS are the emerging dominant organisms | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)

calibration: Must-have — the WHO definition with the 42-day window and organ-dysfunction wording, and the Sepsis-3/SOFA ≥2-point criterion (not merely "infection with fever"). Figures that must be exact: "42 days"; "SOFA ≥2"; "septic shock = MAP ≥65 + lactate >2 despite fluids". Common omissions: the specific GAS relative-risk figures, and stating that "all women are at risk" rather than only listing risk factors.

### SECTION 2 — Primary prevention (35 raw → 7.0 scaled)
model: Primary prevention targets both the health system and the individual woman. System measures include adequate staffing/supplies, water-sanitation-hygiene, hand hygiene, and aseptic technique with minimised vaginal examinations. Individual measures include evidence-based antibiotic prophylaxis (caesarean, GBS colonisation, P-PROM, OASIS) and antenatal screening/treatment of bacteriuria, HIV and comorbidities such as anaemia and diabetes, alongside patient education on danger signs and hygiene.

points:
- [3] Robust health-system infrastructure: adequate trained staff, transport, reliable medical supply chain | WHO (2016) / SLCOG [SLCOG-CHECK]
- [2] Regular multidisciplinary skills-and-drills training of staff in recognition and management, ideally interprofessional | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [3] WaSH — clean water, sanitation and hygiene to reduce healthcare-associated infection | WHO WASH in health care facilities (2016)
- [2] Environmental cleaning and decontamination/sterilisation of equipment | WHO (2016)
- [3] Hand hygiene practised by all staff (WHO "five moments") | WHO Hand Hygiene (2009)
- [2] Aseptic/no-touch technique for examinations and procedures; minimise number of vaginal examinations | Best practice, RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Prophylactic antibiotics before caesarean section, given before skin incision | NICE NG192 (2021)
- [2] Intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis for confirmed maternal GBS colonisation | RCOG GTG 36 (2017)
- [2] Erythromycin for preterm prelabour rupture of membranes | NICE NG25 (2015, updated 2022)
- [2] Antibiotic prophylaxis for 3rd/4th-degree perineal tears (OASIS) | NICE NG192 (2021)
- [1] Screen and treat asymptomatic bacteriuria at booking | NICE NG201 (2021)
- [2] Antenatal screening/treatment of HIV, syphilis and endemic infections | SLCOG/FHB antenatal programme [SLCOG-CHECK]
- [2] Correct anaemia and optimise diabetes/comorbidities that raise sepsis risk | NICE guidance / best practice
- [2] Influenza and COVID-19 vaccination — reduces viral sepsis, now a major residual cause of UK maternal sepsis death | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), citing MBRRACE-UK 2019–21
- [2] Educate all pregnant/postpartum women on signs/symptoms of genital tract and GAS infection, and on hygiene (handwashing before/after toilet or changing sanitary towels) | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Health education, safe abortion services and postpartum family planning to reduce unsafe-abortion sepsis | FHB/SLCOG [SLCOG-CHECK]

calibration: Must-have safety points — the antibiotic-prophylaxis indications, asymptomatic-bacteriuria screening, and influenza/COVID vaccination (now explicitly highlighted by RCOG GTG 64 as the single most correctable driver of UK sepsis deaths). Specifics rewarded: "before skin incision" for caesarean prophylaxis; erythromycin (not co-amoxiclav) for P-PROM. Common omissions: vaccination, and patient-facing education on hygiene/red-flag symptoms.

### SECTION 3 — Secondary prevention: recognition, resuscitation & escalation (40 raw → 8.0 scaled)
model: Secondary prevention depends on a high index of suspicion, structured screening with a validated red/amber flag tool and an obstetric-modified early warning chart, and immediate senior review without delaying treatment. The Hour-1/Sepsis-Six bundle — lactate, two sets of blood cultures before antibiotics, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, 30 ml/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/l, and vasopressors if needed — is delivered within the first hour. A lactate of 2 mmol/l triggers senior review and repeat measurement; 4 mmol/l or more triggers critical-care discussion. Refractory or deteriorating patients need explicit ICU-transfer criteria, ongoing MDT input, and consideration of source control, HSV, or IVIG in refractory Gram-positive toxic/necrotising infection.

points:
- [2] Focused history and thorough examination to identify the infection source; consider non-infective mimics (pre-eclampsia, AFLP, TTP) | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Maintain a high index of suspicion in any woman with infection whose condition deteriorates rapidly — "Think Sepsis" | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Key Recommendation
- [1] Repeated presentation to GP/midwife/triage is a "red flag" warranting urgent face-to-face assessment | MBRRACE-UK 2019–21, cited in RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Use a validated sepsis risk-stratification tool with defined Red Flag and Amber Flag criteria | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 5 / UK Sepsis Trust tool
- [1] Red flags: GCS<15/not alert, RR≥25, SpO2<94% on air, HR>130, systolic BP<90, no urine >12h | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 5
- [2] Monitor using an obstetric-modified early warning score (MEOWS), with multidisciplinary escalation via structured SBAR communication | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Institute prompt treatment on suspicion — do not await senior review or investigation results | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Two sets of blood cultures (aerobic + anaerobic) taken sequentially before antibiotics, without delaying treatment | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Measure serum lactate urgently in all women at high risk of sepsis | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Lactate ≥2 mmol/l should trigger immediate senior review, IV fluids and repeat lactate | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Lactate ≥4 mmol/l indicates tissue hypoperfusion and should prompt immediate escalation, including discussion with critical care | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Key Recommendation
- [1] Routine bloods: FBC, coagulation screen, U&E/creatinine, LFTs, CRP, venous blood gas for lactate/glucose | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Prompt imaging (pelvic USS/CT/MRI, CXR) to confirm/localise the source; should not be deferred because of pregnancy | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] IV broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour of recognition, as part of a structured sepsis bundle (Hour-1/Sepsis-Six) | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Key Recommendation; Table 6
- [2] For life-threatening sepsis with unknown sensitivities: piperacillin-tazobactam or meropenem (Gram-negative cover) plus clindamycin (Gram-positive/anaerobic cover and exotoxin suppression) | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Add vancomycin if MRSA is suspected or known | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Empirical antimicrobials reviewed against culture results and de-escalated/switched to oral agents as soon as appropriate | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Consider IV aciclovir as part of second-line therapy if deteriorating despite first-line antibiotics — disseminated HSV can mimic bacterial sepsis | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), following Coroner's Prevention of Future Deaths report
- [2] Fluid resuscitation: 500 ml crystalloid bolus for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/l, repeated as needed; seek critical care input if >2 L given without improvement; hourly urometer for urine output | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Source control is a priority — drainage of pus, removal of infected retained products, or expedited birth where indicated | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [2] Explicit ICU-transfer criteria: refractory hypotension/lactate >4 needing vasopressors; pulmonary oedema or need for ventilation/airway protection; renal replacement therapy; decreased conscious level; multi-organ failure | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Table 8
- [2] Consider necrotising fasciitis if pain is disproportionate to signs, or bruising develops on an infected wound — needs urgent surgical, intensivist and infection-specialist input, and IVIG in refractory Gram-positive toxic/necrotising infection | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)
- [1] Senior obstetrician decides timing/mode of birth in critically ill women if it benefits mother and/or baby; continuous EFM recommended, caution with fetal blood sampling | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), Key Recommendations
- [1] Neonatal review for risk factors/clinical indicators of early-onset infection if mother treated for sepsis intrapartum or within 24h of birth | RCOG GTG 64 (2024) / NICE NG195
- [1] Barrier nursing and infection-control precautions (including notification) for confirmed/suspected iGAS; treat both mother and baby if either has iGAS | RCOG GTG 64 (2024), UKHSA guidance
- [2] Ongoing regular monitoring (MEOWS-guided) to assess response to treatment; reassess VTE risk (sepsis is a VTE risk factor), MDT input, and clear documentation | RCOG GTG 64 (2024)

calibration: Must-have safety points — antibiotics within 1 hour, blood cultures before antibiotics, the exact lactate thresholds (2 and 4 mmol/l) and their differing actions, explicit ICU-transfer criteria, and recognition of necrotising fasciitis/GAS as an evolving emergency. Figures that must be exact: SpO2 <94% (red flag); RR≥25; lactate 2 vs 4 mmol/l; antibiotics within 1 hour; 500 ml boluses. Common omissions: two sets of blood cultures (not one), the specific piperacillin-tazobactam/meropenem + clindamycin ± vancomycin regimen, HSV as a mimic warranting aciclovir on deterioration, and explicit ICU-transfer criteria rather than a vague "escalate to ICU."

## SCHEME_ID: M01-Q2
- topic: Heavy menstrual bleeding — medical and surgical management across reproductive life
- subject: GYN
- questionType: SAQ
- totalMarks: 20
- rawMarks: 100
- schemeVersion: 1.0

### QUESTION
Critically evaluate the medical and surgical management options available for a woman presenting with heavy menstrual bleeding throughout her reproductive life. (100 marks)

### SECTION 1 — Definition, classification & initial assessment (15 raw → 3.0 scaled)
model: Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) is defined pragmatically as excessive blood loss that interferes with a woman's quality of life. Causes are structured using the FIGO PALM-COEIN classification into structural and non-structural categories. Baseline assessment (full blood count, targeted further tests, transvaginal ultrasound and, where indicated, hysteroscopy with endometrial biopsy) determines which management pathway is appropriate, and the plan must be individualised to the woman's age, fertility wishes and findings.
points:
- [1] HMB defined pragmatically as excessive menstrual blood loss affecting physical, social, emotional or material quality of life | NICE NG88 (2018, updated 2021)
- [1] Affects approximately 1 in 3 women at some point in reproductive life | NICE NG88 (2018, updated 2021)
- [2] FIGO PALM-COEIN system used to structure the differential diagnosis | FIGO classification (Munro et al., 2018 revision)
- [1] Structural causes: Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy/hyperplasia | FIGO PALM-COEIN
- [1] Non-structural causes: Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not-yet-classified | FIGO PALM-COEIN
- [2] Full blood count offered to all women presenting with HMB | NICE NG88
- [1] Coagulation screen, ferritin, thyroid function only if history suggests an underlying disorder | NICE NG88
- [2] Transvaginal ultrasound first-line imaging when structural abnormality suspected, uterus palpable abdominally, or medical treatment has failed | NICE NG88
- [2] Outpatient hysteroscopy with endometrial biopsy where suspected endometrial pathology, persistent intermenstrual bleeding, or risk factors for malignancy are present | NICE NG88
- [2] Management must be individualised by age, fertility wishes, fibroid presence/size, comorbidity and patient preference | NICE NG88

### SECTION 2 — Medical management: critical evaluation (35 raw → 7.0 scaled)
model: Medical management is first-line when there is no significant structural pathology. The levonorgestrel intrauterine system is the most effective option and is recommended first if long-term use is acceptable, followed by non-hormonal (tranexamic acid, NSAIDs) or other hormonal options. GnRH analogues are reserved as a third-line/pre-operative measure because of hypo-oestrogenic side effects. Choice should balance efficacy, contraceptive need, fertility wishes and patient preference, with treatment failure prompting escalation to surgery.
points:
- [4] LNG-IUS (52 mg) recommended as first-line medical treatment when no fibroids >3 cm or other significant pathology, and long-term use (≥12 months) is anticipated | NICE NG88
- [3] LNG-IUS reduces menstrual blood loss by up to ~90% by 12 months, more effective than oral agents | Cochrane review; NICE NG88
- [2] Disadvantages: irregular bleeding/spotting especially in first 3–6 months, requires counselling and an insertion procedure | NICE NG88
- [3] Tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic) taken only during menstruation, reduces blood loss by approximately 40–50% | NICE NG88
- [2] Tranexamic acid suitable when contraception is not required; caution advised with personal VTE risk | NICE NG88
- [2] NSAIDs (e.g. mefenamic acid) reduce blood loss and treat dysmenorrhoea but are less effective than tranexamic acid | NICE NG88
- [3] Combined hormonal contraception can be offered if no contraindications, with the added benefit of contraception | NICE NG88
- [2] Cyclical oral progestogens (e.g. norethisterone days 5–26) can reduce HMB but are less effective than LNG-IUS | NICE NG88
- [2] Long-acting injectable progestogens may be considered though evidence base is more limited | NICE NG88
- [3] GnRH analogues are third-line, used for pre-operative fibroid shrinkage or when other options fail/are contraindicated; add-back HRT needed to limit bone loss beyond 6 months | NICE NG88
- [2] Choice of medical therapy must account for fertility wishes, contraceptive need, fibroid size/presence and patient preference | NICE NG88
- [2] Efficacy of medical treatment typically reviewed at around 3 months before deciding to continue or escalate | NICE NG88 [VERIFY]
- [3] Failure of, contraindication to, or patient preference against medical management are indications to consider surgery | NICE NG88
- [2] Significant underlying structural pathology (e.g. large fibroids, polyps) may limit the efficacy of medical treatment alone | NICE NG88

### SECTION 3 — Surgical management: critical evaluation (35 raw → 7.0 scaled)
model: Surgical options range from fertility-conserving procedures (hysteroscopic resection, myomectomy, uterine artery embolisation) to definitive treatments (endometrial ablation, hysterectomy). Second-generation endometrial ablation is preferred over first-generation techniques for its speed and safety, but is unsuitable for women wishing future fertility. Hysterectomy provides guaranteed amenorrhoea but carries the greatest surgical morbidity and should not be offered as a first-line option; route of hysterectomy and pre-operative work-up must be individualised.
points:
- [3] Surgery considered after failed or declined medical management, significant structural pathology, or informed patient choice | NICE NG88
- [3] Hysteroscopic polypectomy or myomectomy for submucosal fibroids/polyps causing HMB, fertility-preserving | NICE NG88
- [4] Second-generation endometrial ablation techniques preferred over first-generation — shorter procedure time, comparable efficacy, improved safety profile | NICE NG88
- [2] Ablation unsuitable for women wishing future fertility; effective contraception still required afterwards as it is not sterilisation | NICE NG88
- [3] Uterine artery embolisation is a fertility-conserving option for fibroid-related HMB avoiding major surgery, though evidence on fertility/pregnancy outcomes after UAE remains limited | RCOG Scientific Impact Paper — Uterine Artery Embolisation, latest edition [VERIFY]
- [3] Myomectomy (open/laparoscopic/hysteroscopic) for women with fibroids wishing to preserve the uterus/fertility, with a recognised risk of fibroid recurrence | NICE NG88
- [4] Hysterectomy is the only guaranteed definitive treatment but should not be used as a first-line option given the morbidity of major surgery | NICE NG88
- [3] Choice of hysterectomy route (vaginal, laparoscopic, abdominal) individualised by uterine size/mobility, associated pathology and surgeon expertise, with vaginal/laparoscopic preferred over open where feasible | RCOG guidance, latest edition [VERIFY]
- [2] Risks of hysterectomy include haemorrhage, infection, visceral (bladder/bowel/ureteric) injury, VTE, anaesthetic risk and permanent loss of fertility | NICE NG88
- [2] Risks of ablation include uterine perforation and, with fluid-distension systems, fluid overload; post-ablation tubal sterilisation syndrome recognised in sterilised women | NICE NG88 [VERIFY]
- [2] Shared decision-making required, with risks, benefits and alternatives discussed and fertility wishes documented before any surgery | NICE NG88; GMC consent guidance
- [2] Pre-operative optimisation: correction of anaemia, thromboprophylaxis assessment, cross-matching as per local protocol | Local unit protocol [SLCOG-CHECK]
- [2] Histological exclusion of endometrial malignancy before ablation or hysterectomy where indicated | NICE NG88

### SECTION 4 — Tailoring across reproductive life-stages & follow-up (15 raw → 3.0 scaled)
model: Management must be tailored to the woman's life stage. Adolescents need screening for bleeding disorders and are managed medically wherever possible; women still wishing to conceive should avoid ablation and hysterectomy; perimenopausal women need a lower threshold for endometrial assessment and may reasonably progress to definitive surgery sooner. Regular follow-up ensures treatment efficacy is reviewed and anaemia is corrected regardless of the option chosen.
points:
- [2] Adolescents with HMB since menarche should be screened for underlying bleeding disorders (e.g. von Willebrand disease) | NICE NG88; RCOG guidance
- [2] In adolescents, medical management is first-line and surgery avoided; LNG-IUS/COCP appropriate, tranexamic acid/NSAIDs for milder presentations | NICE NG88
- [2] Women wishing to conceive should avoid ablation/hysterectomy; tranexamic acid, NSAIDs, or myomectomy/hysteroscopic resection for structural causes preferred | NICE NG88
- [2] Perimenopausal women carry a higher risk of endometrial pathology, warranting a lower threshold for endometrial biopsy/hysteroscopy before treatment | NICE NG88
- [2] LNG-IUS can be used to bridge to menopause and provides endometrial protection if HRT is subsequently needed | NICE NG88
- [2] Women who have completed their family and are approaching menopause with persistent symptoms may reasonably choose definitive surgery earlier | NICE NG88
- [2] Efficacy and side-effects of medical treatment reviewed at 3–6 months, with reassessment if response is inadequate or new symptoms (e.g. intermenstrual bleeding) develop | NICE NG88
- [1] Ongoing correction of iron-deficiency anaemia alongside whichever definitive treatment is chosen | NICE NG88
